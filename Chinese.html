Chinese Traditional Costumes

Hanfu(1046-256 BCE)

Chinese traditional dress, also known as Hanfu, has a long and rich history dating back thousands of years. The Hanfu has undergone many changes and developments over the centuries, influenced by the political, economic, and cultural changes of each era.During the Zhou Dynasty (1046-256 BCE), Hanfu was simple and consisted of a long robe with wide sleeves and a sash. During the Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE), the Hanfu became more elaborate, with colorful silk and embroidered decorations. The Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE) was known for its luxurious and elegant Hanfu, which featured flowing sleeves and voluminous skirts.

Hanfu(960-1279 CE)

In the Song Dynasty (960-1279 CE), Hanfu became more form-fitting and tailored to the body, with a focus on intricate embroidery and designs. During the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644 CE), the Hanfu became more elaborate and layered, with long robes, waistcoats, and various accessories.In the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912 CE), the Hanfu was replaced by the Manchu-style clothing, which featured a jacket and trousers. However, Hanfu still remained popular among the Han Chinese population and was worn for special occasions and ceremonies.

"hanfu" or "qipao(206 BCE - 220 CE)

Chinese traditional girl dress, also known as "hanfu" or "qipao," is a type of dress that has a long history in Chinese culture. The style of the dress has evolved over time, with different dynasties and regions having their own unique designs and traditions.The "hanfu" dress, which originated in the Han Dynasty (206 BCE - 220 CE), consists of a long, flowing skirt and a fitted jacket. The skirt is typically made of silk or other lightweight fabric and can be decorated with intricate patterns or embroidery. The jacket is often short and fitted, with long sleeves that flare out at the wrists. Accessories such as belts, hats, and jewelry are often worn to complete the outfit.

The Qipao(1644-1912 CE)

The "qipao," which originated in the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912 CE), is a form-fitting dress that accentuates the curves of the body. The dress is typically made of silk or other lightweight fabric and has a high collar, short sleeves, and a slit on either side. The dress can be decorated with intricate patterns or embroidery and is often worn with accessories such as high heels and a clutch bag.Today, traditional Chinese girl dresses are still worn for special occasions such as weddings, festivals, and cultural events. Many modern versions of traditional dresses have been designed to suit contemporary tastes while still maintaining the beauty and elegance of the original designs.

During the Han Dynasty (206 BCE - 220 CE), men typically wore long, loose-fitting robes that were made of silk or other lightweight fabrics. The robes had wide sleeves and a high collar and were often adorned with intricate embroidery or painted designs.During the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE), men's dress became more fitted and accentuated the shape of the body. Men wore a long gown called "shenyi" that was made of silk or other lightweight fabric. The shenyi had a Mandarin collar and was adorned with various accessories such as belts, hats, and jewelry.

(1368-1644 CE)

During the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644 CE), men's dress became more elaborate and featured layers of silk and intricate embroidery. Men wore a long robe called "changpao" that had a high collar and long sleeves. The changpao was often adorned with jewelry and accessories such as hairpins, necklaces, and bracelets.During the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912 CE), men's dress became more formal and tailored. The "changshan," a long, loose-fitting gown with a Mandarin collar and a slit on either side, became the standard dress for men. The changshan was typically made of silk and could be decorated with intricate patterns or embroidery.
